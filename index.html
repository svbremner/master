Research

Peer-Reviewed Articles

“Foucault, Kant, and the Metacritical Tradition”
Forthcoming in British Journal for the History of Philosophy

Abstract: While increasing attention has been paid in recent years to the relation between Foucault's conception of critique and Kant’s, much controversy remains over whether Foucault’s most sustained early engagement with Kant, his dissertation on Kant’s Anthropology, should be read as a wholesale rejection of Kant’s views or as the source of Foucault’s late return to ethics and critique. In this paper, I propose a new reading of the dissertation, considering it alongside 1950s-era archival materials of which I advance the first scholarly appraisal. I argue that Foucault manifests a fundamental ambivalence to Kantian anthropology, rejecting it in theoretical terms while embracing its practical (‘pragmatic’) conception of the subject. Furthermore, I take these texts to collectively evidence Foucault’s attempt to situate himself within the anthropological-critical tradition rather than extricating himself from it. If we interpret Foucault to reject this tradition’s appeal to an essentialized, theoretical conception of subjectivity, what remains of anthropology is its inherent practical reflexivity in structure. Thus, I situate Foucault’s conception of ethics as one’s relation to oneself in continuity with this tradition.

Title redacted: Paper on the unity of Kant’s Third Critique

Dissertation Abstract

My dissertation aims to give an account of what I call 'moral conceivability', or the epistemic dimension of moral deliberation. While philosophers such as Langton, Haslanger, and Fricker have advanced important feminist analyses of the normative dimension of knowledge, these interventions often treat morality as fixed: it is often taken for granted that we know what morality is and what it requires of us. Instead, I argue that the relation also goes the other way: patterns of moral response are also subject to what is conceivable in moral terms.

In my first two chapters, I ground the notion of ‘moral conceivability’ in Kant's account of reflective judgment from the Third Critique, whose function is to form new concepts on the basis of experience. What has been missed in much writing on Kantian morality, I argue, is that practical maxims have a reflective dimension: they involve deriving new principles from one's circumstances or situation, and therefore have an irreducibly historical or social aspect. I posit that Kant’s discovery of reflective judgment transformed his late moral philosophy in a way that has so far escaped notice by commentators, obscuring the possibility of the moral account I advance.

In chapters 3 and 4, I extend the notion of moral conceivability to social philosophy and critical theory. In particular, this notion allows us to broaden conceptions of virtue to include taking a critical stance to one's own received moral concepts; while this possibility has been suggested in passing in moral philosophy by authors such as John McDowell and Barbara Herman, its elaboration in the critical theory tradition has not been sufficiently recognized. To show this, I advance two readings of the self-described ethics of Simone de Beauvoir and Michel Foucault, demonstrating how my conception of moral conceivability allows us to situate their socially critical interventions as ethical -- specifically, as positioned along the reflective dimension of practical deliberation.

About

I entered the PhD program at Columbia University in 2015, having earned an A.B. in Philosophy from the University of Chicago in 2014 and having completed a DAAD Research Fellowship at Goethe-Universität Frankfurt in 2015. I work primarily on Kant (particularly the Third Critique and practical philosophy), 20th century European philosophy, and moral/social philosophy.
